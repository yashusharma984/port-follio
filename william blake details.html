William Blake (1757-1827) was an English poet, painter, and printmaker. He was born in London to a family of modest means, and showed early artistic talent. His parents encouraged his artistic pursuits, and at the age of 14, he was apprenticed to an engraver. Blake attended drawing classes at the Royal Academy and developed his skills as an engraver. His early works were influenced by Renaissance art and the poetry of Edmund Spenser and John Milton. In 1782, he married Catherine Boucher, who became his lifelong companion and collaborator.

Throughout his career, Blake wrote and illustrated his own books, often combining text and image in a unique style. His poems explored themes of spirituality, the imagination, and social and political criticism. Blake was critical of the industrialization and materialism of his time, and questioned orthodox religious beliefs. Despite his innovative approach to art and literature, Blake struggled to find recognition during his lifetime. He was largely unknown to the literary establishment, and had to rely on the patronage of a few supporters. Blake's financial situation was often precarious, and he lived in relative obscurity.

In the last years of his life, Blake's work began to receive more attention. He was recognized as a visionary artist and poet, and his influence on later generations of writers and artists is significant. Today, Blake is considered one of the greatest figures of Romanticism in both art and literature. Some of his notable works include "Songs of Innocence and Experience," "The Marriage of Heaven and Hell," and "Jerusalem." Blake's work continues to inspire and captivate audiences with its mystical and visionary qualities, as well as its exploration of the human condition.
